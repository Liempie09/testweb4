Apple Pie

A timeless British dessert - sweet spiced apples in buttery pastry

Historical Background

Apple pie has been a beloved dessert in Britain since medieval times, with the first recorded recipe appearing in 1381. The dish was originally called "pyes" and was made with a variety of fillings, but apples quickly became the most popular choice due to their abundance in British orchards.

During the Tudor period (1485-1603), apple pie became a staple at royal banquets and common households alike. The saying "as English as apple pie" (later adapted to "as American as apple pie") originated during this time, highlighting the dish's deep connection to British culture and identity.

In medieval England, the pastry crust was often thick and served primarily as a container for the filling rather than being eaten itself. This "coffyn" (as it was called) was designed to preserve the fruit filling. Over centuries, the pastry evolved into the flaky, buttery crust we know today, becoming an integral and delicious part of the dessert.

British apple pie traditionally differs from its American counterpart in several ways. It's often served with custard, cream, or ice cream rather than being eaten plain. The British version also tends to use cooking apples like Bramley, which break down during baking to create a soft, fluffy filling with a perfect balance of sweet and tart flavors.

Today, apple pie remains a quintessential British dessert, served in homes, pubs, and restaurants across the country. It represents the best of British baking traditions - simple ingredients transformed into something truly special through time-honored techniques.

Cooking Instructions

1

Make the Pastry

In a large bowl, rub the butter into the flour and salt until it resembles breadcrumbs. Stir in the sugar. Add the beaten egg and enough cold water to form a dough. Wrap in cling film and chill for 30 minutes.

2

Prepare the Filling

In a large bowl, mix the sliced apples with both sugars, cinnamon, nutmeg, lemon juice, and cornflour. Toss well to coat evenly.

3

Roll the Base

Preheat oven to 200¬∞C (400¬∞F). Roll out two-thirds of the pastry on a floured surface to about 3mm thick. Line a 23cm pie dish, leaving some overhang.

4

Fill the Pie

Spoon the apple mixture into the pastry case, mounding it slightly in the center. Don't pack it too tightly as the apples will shrink during baking.

5

Add the Top

Roll out the remaining pastry for the lid. Brush the edges of the base with water, then place the lid on top. Press the edges to seal and trim any excess. Crimp the edges with a fork or your fingers.

6

Glaze and Vent

Brush the top with beaten egg and sprinkle with demerara sugar. Make a few small slits in the center to allow steam to escape.

7

Bake

Bake for 40-45 minutes until the pastry is golden brown and the filling is bubbling through the vents. If the pastry browns too quickly, cover loosely with foil.

8

Serve

Let the pie cool for 15 minutes before serving. Serve warm with custard, vanilla ice cream, or fresh cream. Enjoy this classic British dessert!
